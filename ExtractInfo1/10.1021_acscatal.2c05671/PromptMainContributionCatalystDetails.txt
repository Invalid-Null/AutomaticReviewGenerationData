Propane Dehydrogenation on PtxSny (x, y â‰¤ 4) Clusters on Al2O3(110)

Abstract
With increasing interest in atomically dispersed and sub-nanometer Pt and Ptâ€“Sn cluster catalysts for propane dehydrogenation (PDH), understanding structureâ€“property relationships is of great importance. In this work, we present density functional theory calculations and microkinetic modeling of PDH on eight alumina-supported Ptx (x = 1â€“4) and PtxSny (x = 1â€“3, y = 1 and x = 2, y = 2) model catalysts and elucidate the effects of Pt nuclearity and Sn heteroatoms. Ptx clusters are prone to sintering, and their stability is improved with the addition of Sn. The Ptx activity changes nonmonotonically with cluster size. Pt1 presents the most favorable dehydrogenation barriers, but its PDH rate is highly limited by the blocking of sites by hydrogen. A volcano-like activityâ€“nuclearity relation is found, with Pt2 being the most active cluster. An increase in Pt nuclearity beyond x = 2 leads to lower rates due to increased dehydrogenation barriers. Moderate addition of Sn somewhat depresses the Pt activity, whereas PtxSny clusters with a Pt/Sn ratio of 1:1 become inactive. The Pt3Sn1 cluster exhibits good activity with improved stability compared to Pt3 and Pt2 and could be an experimental target. While Sn promotes propylene desorption, propane hydrogenolysis to C1 and C2 species on single atoms and small Ptx and PtxSny clusters is unfavorable. Low selectivity to propylene seen experimentally should instead stem from other routes.

1. Introduction
ARTICLE SECTIONSJump To
Alumina-supported Ptâ€“Sn catalysts are commercially used for propane (C3H8) dehydrogenation (PDH) to propylene (C3H6). (1,2) To improve selectivity and stability, Ptâ€“Sn tuned particle sizes have been synthesized on various supports. (3âˆ’9) Theoretical and experimental studies have been performed to understand the electronic and structural properties of Ptâ€“Sn and its catalytic properties, such as activity, selectivity, and coke formation. (10âˆ’14)
The mechanism of PDH on Pt(111) and Pt(211) (10,15,16) and its Câ€“C cracking on Pt(111) have been studied using density functional theory (DFT) calculations and microkinetic modeling (MKM). (17) DFT studies on crystalline Pt3Sn1, Pt2Sn1, and Pt1Sn2 extended surfaces (10) have suggested that Sn slows down the C3H8 Câ€“H activation and facilitates C3H6 desorption. The presence of Sn in PtSn bimetallic catalysts reduces the number of nearest-neighbor Pt pairs on which cracking occurs. Furthermore, charge transfer from Sn to Pt lowers the Pt d-band and thus weakens binding. (10,18âˆ’21) DFT studies of small PtSn clusters on SiO2 and Al2O3 supports have shown that Sn promotes cluster stability because of the strong Ptâ€“Sn interactions, as well as the facile desorption of the olefin that minimizes Câ€“C scission and coking. (13,14,22)
Prior simulations have provided valuable insights into the structure and electronic properties of PtSn alloys. However, the connection between the catalytic properties and structure of supported sub-nanometer PtSn clusters remains unclear. The mechanistic insights of PDH on extended PtSn surfaces may not hold for sub-nanometer clusters such as Pt single atoms and few atom PtSn clusters. It has recently emerged that PtSn sub-nanometer clusters have superior PDH performance, including high activity and selectivity and good stability. (3,8,9,13,14,22âˆ’25)
For example, Zhang et al. have synthesized alumina-supported Pt catalysts ranging from highly dispersed single atoms to sub-nanometer clusters to nanoparticles, among which the atomically dispersed Pt showed the highest activity, selectivity, and stability for propane conversion. (9) Gorey et al., (13) Zandkarimi et al., (14) and Li et al. (22) have studied sub-nanometer Pt cluster models composed of 4 and 7 atoms on silica and alumina supports for dehydrogenation reactions. (13,14,22) In their work, temperature-programmed desorption of ethylene and DFT calculations of ethylene desorption suggested weaker alkene binding to Pt in the presence of Sn. However, the effect of Sn on the activity and selectivity is unclear without a full kinetic simulation of the dehydrogenation reaction. Sub-nanometer Pt and PtSn cluster catalysts have also been synthesized on other supports, such as silica, zeolites, and graphene, and been tested for the dehydrogenation of alkanes. (3,8,9,23âˆ’26)
Considering the tremendous interest in atomically dispersed and sub-nanometer metallic catalysts on oxide supports as described above, our work aims to advance structureâ€“property relationships. Here, we probe the role of nuclearity and of Sn in the stability of alumina-supported PtSn clusters and in their PDH activity and selectivity. Alumina is selected as the support because of its well-defined structure, thermal stability, and extensive usages in industry. To that end, we undertook a comprehensive investigation of PDH on PtxSny (x, y â‰¤ 4) clusters on Î³-Al2O3(110) and developed BrÃ¸nstedâ€“Evansâ€“Polanyi (BEP) relationships.
2. Methods
ARTICLE SECTIONSJump To
2.1. Î³-Alumina Surface Model
Supported PtSn clusters were studied on the Î³-alumina(110) surface, which is the most abundantly exposed facet. (27) Al2O3(110) was represented by 1 Ã— 2 and 2 Ã— 2 models with four atomic layers where the bottom layer is fixed in all calculations. The size of the 1 Ã— 2 model was 8.32 Ã— 16.02 Ã— 20 Ã…3, including âˆ¼15 Ã… vacuum space reserved for PtSn clusters and C3H8 reactions on the surface. For the 2 Ã— 2 slab model, the size along the x axis is 16.64 Ã… and other parameters are identical.
2.2. DFT Calculations
Periodic DFT calculations were performed in the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP), (28,29) using plane-wave basis sets with an energy cutoff of 400 eV. Interactions of valence and core electrons were modeled using the projected augmented wave method. (30) The vdW-DF2 functional, a generalized gradient approximation-based exchangeâ€“correlation functional that includes empirical van der Waals corrections, was employed. (31,32) All calculations were spin polarized.
For calculations of reaction mechanisms, the Brillouin zone was sampled on 4 Ã— 2 Ã— 1 and 2 Ã— 2 Ã— 1 Monkhorstâ€“Pack k-point meshes (33) for the 1 Ã— 2 and 2 Ã— 2 models, respectively. Gaussian smearing with a width of 0.1 eV was used. The electronic structure was converged until the energy difference between two self-consistent field iterations was lower than 10â€“7 eV. The geometry optimization convergence criterion was set at 0.02 eV for all structures of intermediates and transition states. Transition states were searched using the nudged elastic band method (34) for approximate structures and then fully optimized using the dimer method. (35)
2.3. Microkinetic Modeling
The PDH process was simulated using a plug flow reactor at 848 K. (9) The catalyst density was calculated using the dimensions of the alumina-supported PtxSny model. The flow rate and catalyst volume were adjusted for conversion. Detailed MKM information is shown in Table S6. All kinetic parameters were obtained using the Python Multiscale Thermochemistry Toolbox (pMuTT) (36) within the harmonic oscillator approximation. The MKM was performed using the CHEMKIN package. (37)

The coordination geometries of Pt are a triangular or quadrangular pyramid for different PtxSny clusters with a Pt atom at the apex of the pyramid. For example, in Pt1, the Pt atom is coordinated with O1, O2, AlIII, and AlIV, and this Pt is above the plane composed of these four O and Al atoms, leading to a quadrangular pyramid geometry with Pt at the apex. A similar planar geometry is also found for the Pt coordination geometry for other clusters.

3.3. Reaction Mechanisms
Usually, PDH mechanistic studies are limited to a skeleton reaction network involving propane adsorption, two dehydrogenations, and product (propylene and hydrogen) desorption. (10,52) Such a skeleton network does not consider deeper dehydrogenation and hydrogenolysis paths. Scheme 1 presents pathways for the skeleton PDH network to C3H6. We augment it with C3H6 dehydrogenation to C3H4; Câ€“C cleavage in C3H6 to CH2* and CHCH3*; and Câ€“C cleavage in C3H4 to CH* and CCH3* surface species. Hydrogenation of CH* and CH2* yields CH4, and that of CCH3* and CHCH3* yields C2H6. The species undergoing Câ€“C bond scission have been selected for DFT calculations based on prior work where extensively dehydrogenated species undergo faster Câ€“C bond scission. (17,53,54) We studied these reactions for all PtxSny clusters using DFT and MKM. A more comprehensive reaction network than that in Scheme 1 was considered below for Pt1, Pt2, and Pt2Sn1.

3.3.1. C3H8 Dehydrogenation to C3H6
Figure 3 presents the free energy profiles for C3H8 dehydrogenation to C3H6 on Ptx (x = 1â€“4), Pt1Sny (y = 0, 1), Pt2Sny (y = 0â€“2), and Pt3Sny (y = 0, 1). As shown in Figure 3a, both the Câ€“H activation and the ensuing Î²-H elimination face increasingly higher barriers with increasing Ptx cluster size. On Pt1 and Pt2, we see stable surface catalyst states, with formation energies of âˆ’1.01 and âˆ’0.57 eV, that could be responsible for high surface coverages and, thus, few available Pt-free sites for reaction. Strong adsorption is less likely on Pt3 and Pt4. Sn increases the barriers for Câ€“H activation and Î²-H elimination on Pt1 from 0.55 to 2.76 eV and from 0.51 to 2.75 eV, respectively, in essence rendering Pt of Pt1Sn1 rather inactive. Similarly, adding Sn to Pt2 increases the dehydrogenation barriers. From Pt2 to Pt2Sn1 and Pt2Sn2, the two dehydrogenation barriers increase from 1.08 to 2.24 and 2.61 eV and from 0.62 to 2.41 and 3.08 eV, respectively. While Pt2Sn2 is rather inactive, Pt2Sn1 may exhibit some activity because of a moderate 2.41 eV barrier. It is also worth noting that on Pt2Sn1, a stable surface state (Ptxâ€“2H*) with a formation energy of âˆ’0.57 eV is also found, potentially leading to a high probability of observing this state. Addition of Sn to Pt3 increases the first dehydrogenation barrier from 1.59 to 1.97 eV. The pattern that transpires is that the effect of a single Sn atom on increasing the activation energy is diminished as the size of Ptx grows. Overall, one expects the activity of the PtxSny clusters to decrease in the order Pt1 > Pt1Sn1, Pt2 > Pt2Sn1 > Pt2Sn2, and Pt3 > Pt3Sn1. The decreased activity of Pt with adding Sn is consistent with the prior literature on PDH on both extended PtSn surfaces and small PtSn clusters. (10,22)

Interestingly, the PDH TOF is very sensitive to the cluster size; it varies by nearly five orders of magnitude over the range of nuclearity values x = 1â€“4. The TOF trend with nuclearity differs from that with the energy barriers. A volcano-type picture emerges showing that Pt2 is very active and Pt3 is roughly an order of magnitude less so, whereas by comparison Pt1 and Pt4 are significantly less active, by about 5 orders of magnitude relative to Pt2. Pt2 presents the highest TOF as it strikes a balance between low activation barriers, the strength of binding of intermediates (Figure 5a), and facile H2 recombination and desorption (Figure 5b), efficiently turning intermediates over to gas products. The TOF decreases with increasing cluster size (Pt3 and Pt4) because of the higher reaction barriers and the poor stabilization of intermediates. For comparison purposes, we note that the PDH TOF on Pt1/Al2O3 (9.35 Ã— 10â€“2 sâ€“1) is 2â€“3 orders of magnitude larger than those simulated for Pt-doped Ga2O3 (1.78 Ã— 10â€“4 sâ€“1), ZnO (2.52 Ã— 10â€“4 sâ€“1), and Cr2O3 (6.28 Ã— 10â€“5 sâ€“1). (55,56) The decrease in TOF for Ptx (x = 2â€“4) is in line with the experimentally reported trend (at the same temperature) that the TOF decreases with increasing Pt cluster size on alumina. (9,57) When one Sn atom is introduced into Ptx (x = 1â€“3), the TOF decreases by several orders of magnitude. With more Sn, the activity further decreases, as shown by the TOFs of Pt2Sn1 and Pt2Sn2. The trend of decreasing activity with Sn content is consistent with the increased barriers shown in Figure 3.

As shown in Figure 5c, increasing the Ptx cluster size leads to less stable adsorbed species. Addition of Sn generally makes adsorption on Pt less favorable, as reflected in Pt1Sn1, Pt2Sn2, and Pt3Sn1, where the energies of the surface states are all around or above 0. This indicates that the surface should not be significantly covered by adsorbed species. Only on Pt2Sn1 do we see strong binding of both hydrogen (2H-Pt* species resulting after propene desorption) and of the ethyleneâ€“hydrogen complex (C3H6â€“2H-Pt*). Stable species can limit the number of Pt sites available for propane activation. The combined effect of activation barriers and site coverages on activity is quantitatively analyzed next.
Figure 6 shows the coverages of major surface states on Ptx and PtxSny clusters. We see the hydrogen coverage of Pt1 is over 99.9%, which is essentially the reason Pt1 is so much less active than Pt2 even though it presents the lowest dehydrogenation barriers among all the clusters tested here. On Pt2, there are about 2% empty Pt sites available for propane activation. The moderate amount of available Pt sites and small dehydrogenation barriers contribute to its high activity. The surface of Pt2Sn1 is progressively covered by H atoms along the reactor, leading to low TOF when conversion is around 3%. All other clusters have mainly empty Pt sites. Their low activity results from their weak binding and/or large barriers. Significant surface coverages were observed for Pt1, Pt2, and Pt2Sn1, which have stable adsorbed species, such as C3H6â€“2H-Pt* or 2H-Pt*, as shown in Figure 5.

The microkinetic degree of rate control (DRC) of intermediates, products, and transition states is shown in Figure 7. On Pt1, the rate is limited by stable surface species Ptx-2H* and the strong binding of H atoms, as indicated by the negative DRC, namely, the surface coverage influences the rate negatively. The desorption of C3H6 and H2 also limits the rate, as shown by the positive DRC values. On Pt2, the rate is limited by surface coverage in stable C3H6â€“Ptx-2H* species and by C3H6 desorption. Similar to 2H-Pt* on Pt1, significant coverage of C3H6â€“Ptx-2H* on Pt2 leads to negative DRC. Neither on Pt1 nor on Pt2 are the rates limited by dehydrogenation. In contrast, the rates on Pt3 and Pt4 are mainly limited by the two dehydrogenation barriers (TS1 and TS2). The 2H-Pt* species also controls the Pt3 DRC, albeit to a lower degree, consistent with the partial coverage of Pt3. For PtxSny clusters, the rates of Pt1Sn1, Pt2Sn2, and Pt3Sn1 are only limited by the dehydrogenation barriers (TS1 and TS2), while the rate of Pt2Sn1 is limited by the binding of H atoms, TS2, and H2 desorption.

In summary, we find that the C3H8 dehydrogenation rate is not always determined by a single step. Instead, it can be influenced by dehydrogenation and desorption steps as well as surface coverages of stable species. The key elementary step(s) and adsorbed state(s) change dramatically with nuclearity for these clusters. Increasing Pt nuclearity of Ptx clusters leads to less stable catalyst states and higher dehydrogenation barriers, shifting the rate-limiting steps from surface states to high barriers. Other than Pt2Sn1, the addition of Sn leads to less active Pt and, therefore, less stable surface states. Hence, rates are mainly limited by dehydrogenation transition states.

3.4. C3H6 Dehydrogenation and Câ€“C Cracking
In this section, we focus on PtxSny clusters for x and y â‰¤ 2; larger clusters, such as Pt3, Pt3Sn1, and Pt4, were not considered due to intractable combinatorial challenges related to the possible reaction pathways. As shown above, Pt1Sn1 and Pt2Sn2 are inactive, while Pt1, Pt2, and Pt2Sn1 show activity. These catalysts are further studied for deep dehydrogenations and Câ€“C cleavages to probe the selectivity to C3H6.
Figure 9 shows the free energy profiles for further dehydrogenation of C3H6 to C3H4 on Pt1, Pt2, and Pt2Sn1. On Pt1 and Pt2, the dehydrogenation barriers for C3H6 are close to those of C3H8. The Câ€“C cleavage of C3H6 on Pt1 is more facile than dehydrogenation. In contrast, on Pt2Sn1, both dehydrogenation and Câ€“C cleavage of C3H6 are energetically more demanding. However, our calculations indicate that Câ€“C cleavage of C3H4 on Pt1 and Pt2Sn1 is quite unlikely; in contrast, it is not as unlikely as on Pt2. Addition of Sn suppresses C3H6 binding with Pt and increases the dehydrogenation and cleavage barriers. The suppression of alkene binding potentially leads to less coke, suggested by similar suppression of C2H4 binding on Pt4Sn3 compared to Pt4 on silica. (13) The role of Sn hindering C3H6 conversion is akin to the extended PtSn surfaces and other heteroatoms to Pt such as Zn. (10,52) Further hydrogenation steps for Câ€“C cleavage to CH3CHâ€“Ptâ€“CH2, leading to C2H6 and CH4, were calculated on Pt1, Pt2, and Pt2Sn1 (Figure S20). It is found that H2 adsorption is energetically very demanding, making these steps unfavorable, although after H2 adsorption, the hydrogenations are thermodynamically downhill.

The effect of Sn on hindering C3H6 conversion leads to high C3H6 selectivity as well as less coking from cracking. This agrees well with experimentally observed high C3H6 selectivity and good catalytic stability. For example, the Pt3Sn1 alloy surface of Ptâ€“Sn/SBA-15 contributed 92% to propylene selectivity, in contrast to less than 70% selectivity for reaction over Pt/SBA-15. Figure 10 shows the free energy barriers of Câ€“C cleavage of C3H6 and C3H4 on Pt1, Pt2, and Pt2Sn1 clusters. For C3H6 cleavage, Pt2 has a slightly higher barrier than mononuclear Pt, while addition of Sn increases the barrier by more than 1.5 eV. A similar increase of the cleavage barrier, when Sn is added to the Pt dimer, is also observed for C3H4 cleavage. C3H4 cleavage is not as favorable as C3H6 on Pt1. Overall, an increase in nuclearity leads to a slight rise in C3H6 cleavage energy. The addition of Sn makes both C3H6 and C3H4 cleavage much more difficult. The latter finding is consistent with the hypothesized role of Sn in Pt and the selectivity of PDH. What is surprising is that one would expect that monomers and dimers do not support hydrogenolysis and that larger clusters enable more facile Câ€“C bond scission due to the ensemble effect. For these small clusters, the DFT data do not support this intuition. It is unclear at what size Câ€“C becomes relevant. We expand on this point below using MKM and semi-empirical methods.

On Pt2Sn1, no cleavage is observed. C3H6 desorption is not in equilibrium on Pt1 and Pt2, which is also one of the rate-controlling steps, as shown in the sensitivity analysis (Figure 7). On Pt2Sn1, the second dehydrogenation is not in equilibrium, which is also one of the rate-controlling steps. On all three clusters, we do not see much C3H6 dehydrogenation to C3H4. 4. Conclusions
ARTICLE SECTIONSJump To
In this work, we investigated the effects of cluster size and Sn content on cluster stability and PDH on PtxSny (x, y â‰¤ 4) clusters supported on Î³-Al2O3(110). The stability of Pt clusters increases with increasing nuclearity. The addition of Sn also stabilizes Pt clusters due to the strong Ptâ€“Sn interaction. It is also favorable for Ptx clusters to bind with PtxSn1 clusters and form larger PtxSn1 clusters. Sn promotes Pt1Sn1 dispersion on alumina.
Although the dehydrogenation energy barriers increase with increasing Ptx cluster size, the reactivity is strongly dependent on the stability of surface species. Consequently, the activity exhibits a volcano curve with nuclearity, maximized for Pt2 whose activity is significantly high (a magic cluster-like behavior). The activity of Pt1, which exhibits the lower dehydrogenation barrier, is much lower than those of larger Pt clusters due to high H coverage. For Ptx (x > 1), the reactivity decreases with increasing cluster size, correlating with the calculated dehydrogenation barriers. The apparent reaction kinetics result from the interplay of reaction barriers and stability of intermediates and vary profoundly with nuclearity. The introduction of Sn lowers the reactivity of Pt, as has been reported in previous work. Increasing the Sn content leads to even lower reactivity. High dehydrogenation barriers and low TOFs on Pt1Sn1 and Pt2Sn2 suggest that a Pt/Sn ratio of 1:1 results in inactivity. When there is an excess of Pt atoms relative to Sn in PtxSny (e.g., Pt2Sn1 and Pt3Sn1), moderate TOF values were observed. For small clusters, it seems that a much lower Sn percent than an equimolar ratio, e.g., Pt3Sn1, gives higher activity. Furthermore, Pt1 promotes slight deep C3H6 conversion to cracked products, while Pt2 and Pt2Sn1 maintain high selectivity to C3H6. While the addition of Sn promotes C3H6 desorption, consistent with prior work, the microkinetics do not indicate hydrogenolysis on these small clusters, and thus, we expect cluster catalysts to be selective to olefins irrespective of Sn addition. Pt2 is the most active among the studied clusters; its TOF is very high, but it may suffer from stability. Pt3Sn1 seems to strike a better balance of enhanced stability with activity and should be a good experimental target.
