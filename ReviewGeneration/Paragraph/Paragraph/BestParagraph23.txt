Based on the detailed analysis from the references provided [10.1016/j.cej.2022.140726], [10.1038/s41929-023-00968-7], [10.1038/s41929-023-00974-9], [10.1038/nmat4757], and [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816], zeolites and mesoporous materials have shown promise as supports for encapsulating metal species to enhance the stability of propane dehydrogenation (PDH) catalysts. 

Several key examples demonstrate that strategically localizing metal clusters like platinum or encapsulating sub-nanometric bimetallics within zeolite pores and channels prevents agglomeration and sintering during high temperature PDH conditions or regeneration cycles:

- Co species anchored on BEA-type zeolites exhibited no deactivation over 8 reaction-regeneration PDH cycles, enabled by the confinement effect stabilizing metal clusters [10.1016/j.cej.2022.140726]. 

- Pt anchored on germanium sites in UTL-type zeolites maintained exceptional stability for over 4000 hours of PDH, attributed to Pt-O-Ge bonding interactions localizing platinum [10.1038/s41929-023-00968-7].

- Sub-nanometric PtSn species regioselectively positioned inside MFI-type zeolites preserved cycle stability for at least 3 PDH cycles without changes in cluster size or deactivation [10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7].

- Encapsulating platinum inside the micropores of MCM-22 zeolite prevented agglomeration after extreme oxidation-reduction treatments at 650°C. The encapsulated catalyst retained 90% initial PDH activity after 5 cycles while a conventional impregnated catalyst lost over 40% [10.1038/nmat4757].

In summary, strategic zeolite encapsulation techniques emerge as a promising approach to enhance metal dispersion and stabilization for long-term PDH operation. Anchoring guest metals on zeolite sites or confinement within micropores mitigates sintering processes to maintain active sites and initial activity over repeated use. Further developments in zeolite composites and mesoporous architectures may unlock additional opportunities for next-generation encapsulation methods.
