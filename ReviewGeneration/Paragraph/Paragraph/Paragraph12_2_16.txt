Supported mono-metallic Pt, Pd, and Ir catalysts have been explored for propane dehydrogenation, with Pt-based catalysts being among the most widely studied and commercially used. 

Several studies evaluate alumina-supported Pt catalysts, providing insights into activity, selectivity, and deactivation mechanisms. Adding promoters such as 1 wt% boron can reduce coking and improve selectivity [Placeholder_Of_DOI2]. Temperature-programmed oxidation profiles identified different coke types on spent Pt/Al2O3 catalysts [Placeholder_Of_DOI4].

Beyond Al2O3, other supports have been probed including boron nitride (BN) [Placeholder_Of_DOI5] and germanium-substituted UTL zeolite (Ge-UTL) [Placeholder_Of_DOI6]. Data shows Pt/BN maintains conversion better than Pt/Al2O3 with near 100% propylene selectivity. Confining Pt clusters in Ge-UTL 14-membered ring channels via Pt‒O‒Ge anchoring leads to ultrastable single atoms.

While most studies focus on Pt, Ru and Rh catalysts are also beginning to be explored. At similar conversion levels, supported Ru nanoparticles showed far lower selectivity than Pt, but Ru phosphides reached >90% propylene selectivity [Placeholder_Of_DOI7]. A supported RhCu single atom alloy catalyst demonstrated higher activity, 100% propene selectivity and coking resistance compared to a standard Pt/Al2O3 catalyst [Placeholder_Of_DOI8].

Overall, Pt-based catalysts remain the foremost choice for selective propane dehydrogenation, with ongoing efforts to enhance stability and minimize deactivation via modified supports, promoters, and controlled metal dispersion.

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The studies compiled underscore supported Pt as the conventional catalytic material for propane dehydrogenation, especially on Al2O3, with nascent endeavors probing alternative metals like Ru and promoters to further augment catalytic performance. As research momentum in single atom and cluster catalysis intensifies in allied domains, an opportunity exists to extend such frameworks to propane dehydrogenation by advancing ultra-dispersed Pt catalysts integrated with unconventional supports. Exploring interfacial phenomena through advanced electron microscopy and spectroscopy techniques could provide mechanistic clarity. Operando spectroscopic monitoring coupled with kinetic isotopic labeling and DFT calculations can offer insights into the nature of active sites during working state dehydrogenation. Endeavors to prolong cycle life via bespoke catalyst regeneration methods also merit attention.
