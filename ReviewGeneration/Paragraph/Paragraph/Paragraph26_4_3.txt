Based on the details extracted from the file, the article with DOI 10.1021/jacs.2c02636 employs integrated differential phase contrast scanning transmission electron microscopy (iDPC-STEM) to characterize the Co-support interface in propane dehydrogenation catalysts. Specifically, iDPC-STEM images reveal that isolated Co atoms with a distorted tetrahedral coordination are homogenously embedded into the T1(7) and T3(9) sites of MFI zeolites [DOI 10.1021/jacs.2c02636]. 

Additionally, the article with DOI 10.1038/s41563-019-0430-4 uses a combination of high-resolution, high-angle annular dark-field STEM (HR HAADF-STEM) and integrated differential phase contrast (iDPC) imaging. This advanced microscopy approach enables precise localization of subnanometric Pt and PtSn clusters preferentially within the sinusoidal channels of the MFI zeolite structure [DOI 10.1038/s41563-019-0430-4].

Moreover, the article with DOI 10.1021/acscatal.3c00816 employs advanced iDPC-STEM microscopy to directly visualize the coordination environment of incorporated Co species within the S-1 zeolite framework. Images reveal the distorted sites that the Co atoms occupy within the framework [DOI 10.1021/acscatal.3c00816].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The advanced microscopy techniques discussed demonstrate the tremendous potential to gain insights into metal-support interfacial sites at the atomic level in propane dehydrogenation catalysts. As these pioneering studies reveal, critical active sites may reside in specific locations within the support framework. 

Future research could explore extending these microscopy methods to additional catalyst compositions and reaction conditions. New capabilities in operando microscopy under working environments may illuminate how interfacial sites evolve during propane dehydrogenation. By correlating atomic-scale imaging with kinetic and spectroscopic data, research endeavors could pinpoint definitive active site structures.

Ultimately, these microscopic perspectives may guide the rational design of optimized catalysts, where controlling the metal-support interface facilitates superior activity, selectivity, and stability.
