Based on the details extracted from the references, propane dehydrogenation (PDH) faces considerable thermodynamic constraints due to being a highly endothermic process. As stated in [10.1021/acscatal.3c00103], the standard reaction enthalpy is 124.3 kJ/mol, necessitating high temperatures of 500-600°C to achieve sufficient conversion thermodynamically [10.1021/acscatal.1c01924, 10.1021/acscatal.0c01455]. The equilibrium conversion limits performance, with conversion enhanced by lowering propane partial pressures [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331].

Several articles discuss kinetic factors as well. The rate-limiting step is identified as H2 formation in [10.1021/acscatal.0c01455]. Activation barriers around 0.7-1.76 eV are reported for elementary steps like initial C-H bond activation [10.1021/acscatal.3c00889, 10.1021/acscatal.0c03999]. Turnover frequencies, selectivities, and reaction orders are determined experimentally to elucidate kinetics [10.1021/acscatal.3c00939]. Apparent activation energies of 180-200 kJ/mol for propane dehydrogenation are calculated, showing the kinetic energy inputs needed [10.1021/jacs.2c03941].

First principles DFT calculations provide additional insights. Correlations are found between the adsorption energies of key intermediates and transition states, shedding light on potential rate-determining steps [10.1021/acscatal.3c00889]. Several articles note the tradeoff between desired dehydrogenation activity and undesired cracking/coking side reactions, which depend on factors like site isolation vs. ensemble requirements [10.1016/j.jechem.2023.04.019, 10.1021/acscatal.1c01916].
