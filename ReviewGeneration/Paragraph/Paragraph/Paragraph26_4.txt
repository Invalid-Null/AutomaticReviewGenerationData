Based on the details extracted from the file, advanced microscopy techniques have revealed critical insights into metal-support interfaces in propane dehydrogenation catalysts. Specifically, aberration-corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (AC-STEM) techniques like high-angle annular dark field imaging (HAADF-STEM) and integrated differential phase contrast (iDPC) have enabled atomic-scale visualization of isolated metal atoms and clusters preferentially bonded to targeted sites within zeolitic frameworks [10.1021/jacs.2c02636, 10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7]. By correlating iDPC images delineating the microporous structure with HAADF-STEM imaging sensitive to heavy metal species, the precise siting and coordination environment of sub-nanometric Pt, Sn, and Co motifs anchored to the T1/T3 sites of MFI and BEA topologies via (SiO)2M(HOSi)2 linkages have been discerned [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816, 10.1021/jacs.2c02636]. Aberration-corrected electron microscopy has further unlocked the intricate atomic configurations of intermetallic PtZn and PtY nanoparticles exhibiting L12 superlattices and (110) terminated facets when supported on mesoporous zeolites [10.1038/s41586-020-2671-4]. The strong metal-support interaction imposed by the zeolite significantly restricts sintering, with AC-STEM tracking negligible changes in Pt cluster dimensions after propane dehydrogenation [10.1002/anie.201805150]. Meanwhile, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and SEM-EDX elemental mapping has enabled characterization of metal spatial distributions across hierarchical porous networks, affirming uniform dispersion of active Ga and Zn species on alumina and silica matrices [10.1016/j.jechem.2019.04.027, 10.1016/j.jechem.2021.04.034]. By furnishing direct visualization of isolated sites and intimate interfaces governing catalyst reactivity and stability, electron microscopy developments are propelling molecular-level optimization pathways for propane dehydrogenation systems.
