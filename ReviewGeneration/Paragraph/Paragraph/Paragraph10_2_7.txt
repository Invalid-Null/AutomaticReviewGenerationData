Based on the details extracted from the references, several mechanistic scenarios have been proposed for propane dehydrogenation over different metal oxide catalysts.

On Cr-based catalysts, active sites have been identified as Cr3+ species [DOI1, DOI2], specifically mono-nuclear Cr3+ with coordinative vacancies [DOI3]. The reaction mechanism is suggested to involve redox cycles between Cr3+/Cr2+ or Cr6+/Cr3+/Cr2+ species [DOI4]. 

For V-based catalysts, studies show involvement of isolated V species like VOx [DOI5], V3+ [DOI6], and AlOV species [DOI7], typically going through Mars-van Krevelen type pathways utilizing redox cycles between higher and lower V oxidation states. Lattice oxygen assists in oxidative dehydrogenation while reduced V3+ catalyzes subsequent non-oxidative steps after lattice oxygen depletion [DOI8].

Over ZrO2, DFT calculations indicate two Zr cations with unsaturated coordination (Zrcus) located at an oxygen vacancy serve as active sites [DOI9, DOI10]. They break propane's C-H bonds homolytically via hydrogen abstraction, leading to a Zrcus-C3H7 intermediate that eliminates H2 upon dehydrogenation.  

For Ga-containing catalysts, isolated, monovalent Ga+ sites are identified as the active centers responsible for dehydrogenation [DOI11, DOI12]. A heterolytic dissociation pathway is preferred over a redox route, with key steps involving hydride extraction to form unsaturated intermediates [DOI13].

In summary, the proposed scenarios describe specific active sites like isolated cations, cation-oxygen pairs, or cation clusters that activate propane through oxidative, non-oxidative, or dehydrogenative steps. Common intermediates include alkyl species, while redox cycles enable lattice oxygen regeneration.
