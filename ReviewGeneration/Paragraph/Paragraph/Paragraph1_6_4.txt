Propylene is a crucial chemical feedstock with widespread applications in the production of polypropylene, acrylonitrile, propylene oxide, and other chemicals [Placeholder_of_10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.025, Placeholder_of_10.1021/acscatal.3c00816]. There is a large and growing supply-demand gap for propylene that is projected to persist over the next decade, necessitating alternative production routes beyond conventional naphtha and heavy oil cracking [Placeholder_of_10.1021/acscatal.3c00816]. Propane dehydrogenation (PDH) has emerged as a promising technology to produce propylene by exploiting abundant and low-cost supplies of propane from shale gas [Placeholder_of_10.1016/j.jcat.2021.09.008, Placeholder_of_10.1021/acscatal.3c00939]. PDH is an economically competitive and environmentally friendly way to generate propylene on purpose [Placeholder_of_10.1021/acscatal.3c00889].
※※※ 
Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The growing propylene supply deficit highlights the need for alternative production routes to meet increasing chemical industry demand. Propane dehydrogenation stands out as an attractive option based on low propane feedstock costs from shale gas and more environmentally-friendly operations. Moving forward, research can focus on improving PDH catalyst design and reactor engineering to drive further advances. Opportunities exist to explore hybrid processes coupling PDH with other technologies to boost yields. Broader adoption of renewable propane feeds via gasification or fermentation could also expand PDH's footprint.
