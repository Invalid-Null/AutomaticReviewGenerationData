Propane dehydrogenation (PDH) is an industrially important reaction that produces propylene, a key building block for polymers and chemicals. Zeolites and mesoporous materials have emerged as promising supports to encapsulate and stabilize metal-based PDH catalysts, addressing key challenges like sintering and coking that lead to deactivation. This review analyzes recent advances in metal encapsulation strategies using zeolites and related porous structures to enhance PDH catalyst stability.

**Zeolites and Mesoporous Supports - How does the encapsulation of metals enhance stability?**

Metals and metal clusters are the active sites for catalyzing dehydrogenation reactions in PDH. However, unsupported metals are prone to agglomeration and growth into larger, less active nanoparticles during high temperature PDH conditions [\[10.1016/j.jcat.2020.02.019\]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jcat.2020.02.019). Encapsulating these metal species inside zeolites and mesoporous hosts stabilizes the active sites. The microporous crystalline framework acts as a physical barrier that prevents diffusion and coalescence ([\[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103\]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acscatal.3c00103)). This confinement effect maintains high dispersion of sub-nanometric clusters and even isolated atoms. 

Various zeolite topologies have been utilized to encapsulate common PDH metals like Pt, Zn, and Sn. Strategies include hydrothermal synthesis with metal precursors to grow zeolites around templated metal sites ([\[10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acscatal.3c00816)), and post-synthetic techniques like solid-state ion exchange and impregnation for existing zeolites. PtSn nanoclusters confined in MFI ([\[10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7\]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7)), PtZn in S-1 ([\[10.1021/acscatal.1c01808\]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acscatal.1c01808)), and Pt in UTL ([\[10.1038/s41929-023-00968-7\]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41929-023-00968-7)) exhibited high long-term stability under industrially relevant PDH conditions. No obvious deactivation was observed even after thousands of hours, unlike conventional supported catalysts. 

The zeolite framework additionally provides Br√∏nsted acid sites that anchor partially reduced metal species during cycling, facilitating re-oxidation back to active cations ([\[10.1016/j.jechem.2021.04.034\]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jechem.2021.04.034)). This regeneration capability maintains performance over repeated reaction-regeneration sequences. Stable metal-zeolite interaction is integral, with germanium playing an emerging role for strong metal anchoring ([\[10.1038/s41929-023-00968-7\]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41929-023-00968-7)). Overall, zeolite encapsulation addresses key stability issues in PDH catalyst design.
