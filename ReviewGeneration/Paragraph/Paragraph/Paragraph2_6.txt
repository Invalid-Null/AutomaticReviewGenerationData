	<English>
		Industrial propane dehydrogenation (PDH) processes utilize supported Pt and CrOx catalysts to selectively activate C-H bonds in propane over C-C bonds, resulting in dehydrogenation to propylene instead of undesired cracking reactions [10.1016/j.jcat.2021.09.008]. The catalyst active sites preferentially break C-H bonds due to geometric and electronic factors [10.1016/j.jechem.2023.04.019, 10.1021/acscatal.1c01916]. Alternative catalysts like VOx/SiO2 also facilitate dehydrogenation through lower energy redox mechanisms rather than direct C-H cleavage [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999]. Introducing supports like mesoporous silica allows tuning of active site geometry and stabilization [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999]. Overall, catalysts overcome the high C-H bond strength of 409 kJ/mol and associated activation barriers that limit thermal PDH [10.1016/S1872-2067(18)63196-1].
		※※※
		The findings showcase how rational catalyst design, using principles like confinement effects and geometric control of active sites, can unlock the potential of PDH for on-purpose propylene production. As computational methodologies continue advancing in tandem with operando characterization techniques, opportunities exist to further optimize activation barriers through precise atomic-scale engineering. Broader implementation of these highly selective, stabilized catalyst systems could support the shifting feedstock landscape and growing propylene demands.
