<English>
Despite decades of research and commercialization efforts, thermally induced deactivation remains a major issue limiting the stability and lifespan of propane dehydrogenation catalysts [10.1021/acscatal.3c00103]. Even long-standing industrial catalysts based on platinum and chromium oxide still face drawbacks like high costs and environmental concerns, driving interest in alternative non-precious metal formulations [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816]. However, developing replacements that match the efficiency of conventional catalysts continues to pose difficulties. 

Beyond stability improvements, increasing propylene selectivity and yields also persists as an ongoing pursuit in propane dehydrogenation catalyst development [10.1021/acscatal.3c00103]. Gaining a predictive understanding of how factors like active site architecture and local coordination environment influence selectivity has remained challenging [10.1038/s41929-022-00885-1]. Furthermore, the fundamental mechanisms of propane dehydrogenation over many relevant catalysts are still not comprehensively mapped out. For example, the intrinsic effects of promoters like gallium and oxygen vacancies on catalyst performance are debated, with calls for additional spectroscopic investigations into transient reaction intermediates [10.1021/acscatal.1c05724, 10.1021/acscatal.1c02621].

Looking forward, new in-situ characterization techniques and computational modeling methods may assist in clarifying outstanding mechanistic questions surrounding propane dehydrogenation [10.1021/cr5002436]. Exploring alternative preparation strategies to stabilize isolated single atoms and atom clusters also offers promise for potentially boosting efficiency [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331]. Research leveraging these emerging approaches to uncover more selective and robust propane dehydrogenation catalyst formulations remains an ongoing need.
