The Journal of Physical Chemistry C Virtual Special Issue on “Energy and Catalysis in China”
 SPECIAL ISSUE
Published as part of The Journal of Physical Chemistry virtual special issue “Energy and Catalysis in China”.
Energy plays a central role in our modern society. Due to the growing energy crisis and environmental concerns, seeking clean and sustainable energy sources is becoming one of the most important global issues and has attracted the increasing interest of researchers from all over the world. In particular, research about catalysis in energy, including electrocatalysis, thermocatalysis, and photocatalysis, is an important direction, as it can greatly improve the efficiency of energy storage and conversion or may even create new ways to utilize energy. At the same time, electrochemical energy devices, such as lithium/sodium-ion batteries and fuel cells, have also emerged as highly efficient energy ultilization technologies, especially when being combined with renewable energy power generation. These devices involve complicated physical chemistry processes. Therefore, understanding the fundamental physical chemistry mechanisms of these energy-related processes will promote the development of more efficient functional materials and/or energy devices. During the past decade, researchers in China have made extensive efforts and achieved significant advances in the energy and catalysis field. This Virtual Special Issue (VSI) highlights the recent experimental and theoretical progress made by researchers from mainland China. It includes 112 papers, which cover topics from the development of highly efficient energy materials to in situ/operando characterization, mechanism studies, and theoretical simulations.
Electrocatalysis and electrochemical energy generation/storage are essential for the utilization of renewable energy like solar and wind energy, as they can help to store these intermittent energies as chemical energy. The prototypical electrochemical energy devices are fuel cells and lithium-ion batteries. The oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) is the most critical electrocatalytic reaction in fuel cells due to its slow kinetics. This VSI includes 7 research papers about the development of efficient or stable ORR catalysts, ultrathin palladium/transition-metal alloy nanowires, (1) Au-modified Pt–Co/C, (2) PtnNi tetrahedrons, (3)N,N-di(2-picolyl)ethylenediamine (DPEN)-modified Co corrole, (4) Fe–Nx–C or Co–N4 single-atom catalysts, (5,6) and Co-, N-, and S-tridoped carbon frameworks for ORR (7) as well as a review paper about non-precious-metal electrocatalysts. (8) Moreover, the influence of the microstructures and transport properties of ionomer films on the performance of Pt single atom catalysts (SACs) in polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) has been reported. (9) The development of electrode materials for lithium-ion batteries is included in this VSI: lithium-iron-phosphate-coated LiNixCoyMn1–x–yO2 (NCM) (10) and porous Co3O4 nanoparticles assembled nanostructures for lithium-ion batteries, (11) and N,S-codoped three-dimensional (3D) network carbon materials supported RuO2 for Li–O2 batteries, (12) while Fe-doped PrBa0.8Ca0.2Co2O6−δ double perovskite oxide is reported as an efficient cathode material for solid oxide fuel cells. (13) The understanding of reactive oxygen species in aprotic lithium–oxygen batteries is reviewed. (14) There are also several papers concerning other important/emerging electrocatalytic reactions and energy processes: the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) on a carbon-nanotube-supported NiIrx nanoalloy, (15) NiO/CuO nanosheet, (16) or Ni3S2/Fe(OH)2, (17) the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) on WNi4@W–WO2, (18) CO2 reduction on a 3D porous Zn catalyst, (19) oxygen reduction to hydrogen peroxide on a Nd-doped Bi4Ti3O12 nanosheet, (20) the reduction of N2 to ammonia on a Au/SiO2/Si photocathode, (21) the selective oxidation of methane to ethanol on Rh/ZnO nanosheets, (22) the triiodine reduction reaction on zeolitic-imidazolate-framework-derived Co–N3 SACs, (23) the electrohydrogenation of cinnamate esters on graphite felt electrode, (24) and electricity generation via the hydrovoltaic effect. (25)
Thermocatalysis and Photocatalysis. Thermocatalysis improves the utilization efficiency of traditional fossil energy or reduces the emission of pollutants. In this VSI, various thermocatalytic reactions on catalysts of metals, oxides, carbon materials, zeolites, resins, and others are discussed. These papers can be roughly classified as follows. (1) Energy catalysis: conversion of syngas to higher alcohols on CoMn-modified Cu-based mixed oxides, (26) direct methane conversion to methanol on PdAu nanowires, (27) hydrogenation of CO2 to ethanol on Na-promoted Rh embedded in S-1 zeolites, (28) oxidative coupling of methane on A+1Nb5+O3 (A = Li, Na, K) perovskites, (29) and synthesis of jet fuel from glycerol and tert-butyl alcohol on organic solid acid catalysts. (30) (2) Environmental catalysis: catalytic elimination of soot and NOx on Mn-based perovskites, (31) catalytic dehydrochlorination of dichloroethane on porous carbon, (32) oxidation of propane and CO on reducible oxide-supported Pt or PtCu nanocatalysts, (33,34) complete oxidation of benzene on Pt SACs, (35) preferential oxidation of CO on CuxCe1–xO2 nanorods, (36) CO oxidation on Pd/ZnO catalysts (37) or Au–Fe2O3 interfaces, (38) and selective catalytic reduction of nitrogen oxide with methane on Co-exchanged SSZ-13 zeolite catalysts. (39) (3) Synthesis of fine chemicals: selective reduction of nitrobenzene on iron and nitrogen cofunctionalized carbon materials (40) or PtPdCu/Al2O3, (41) hydrogenation of quinoline and benzoic acid on RhPt/MCM-41, (42) hydroformylation of diisobutene on CoFe alloy catalysts, (43) and selective hydrogenation of cinnamaldehyde to cinnamyl alcohol on Pt@Fe-CeO2 catalysts (44) or Pt/TiO2. (45) Photocatalysis can directly convert solar energy into chemical energy, and two important photocatalytic reactions are included in this VSI: photocatalytic water splitting on Au nanoparticles embedded in g-C3N4, (46) BiVO4, (47) Ta3N5, (48) SnNb2O6 nanoplates, (49) silicon material, (50) MoO3–ZnIn2S4, (51) carbon nitride, (52) NiFe metal–organic framework (MOF) and cobaloxime-modified Ti-doped hematite, (53) and photocatalytic CO2 conversion on α-Fe2O3/CdS heterostructures, (54) CdSe/CdSexS1–x/CdS alloyed quantum dots/TiO2, (55) AgTaO3, (56) perylene diimide/graphene-g-C3N4, (57) P-doped ZnIn2S4, (58) MCo2O4−δ (M = Zn, Ni, Cu), (59) and PtRu/TiO2. (60) A few other photocatalytic reactions, such as the selective oxidation of cyclohexane to cyclohexanone on In2O3/N-doped TiO2, (61) the reduction of aromatic nitro compounds on Ag/AgxS, (62) and NO removal on Ba-doped BiOBr, (63) are also discussed.
In situ characterization can provide fundamental insights into reaction mechanisms and structure–activity relationships of energy processes, which promote the rational design of more efficient energy materials and devices. In this VSI, nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy has been used to investigate the surface acidity of niobium and tantalum oxides (64) and the structure evolution of Na3V2(PO4)3, a typical representative of Na-ion batteries, under different charging and discharging states, (65) as well as the photocatalytic methanol or ethanol reforming reaction on photocatalysts consisting of Pt supported on graphitic carbon nitride. (66) In situ infrared spectroscopy was employed to study the reaction mechanism of electrocatalysis. (67) In situ X-ray absorption spectroscopy was used to study the lithium-ion battery cathode materials (68) and the OER on α-Ni(OH)2 nanobelts. (69) Near-ambient-pressure X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and quasi-in-situ XPS were used to study oxygen vacancies in reduced MnOx (70) and CO activation and hydrogenation on Co(0001), (71) respectively. Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) was used to characterize the self-assembled Pd(II) meso-tetra(4-carboxyphenyl)porphine (PdTCPP) adlayer under electrochemical conditions, (72) the FeO/Cu2O/Cu(100) interfaces during thermal reduction, (73) and the structural changes of FeO/Cu(111) during redox treatments. (74) Differential electrochemical mass spectrometry was used in the study of the stability of organic cation species (R4N+, R = CH3–, C2H5–, and n-C3H7−) during the HER. (75)
Theoretical calculation can directly simulate the chemical and electronic properties of energy materials as well as their interactions with reactants, intermediates, and products. It greatly deepens the understanding of the molecular mechanisms of various energy processes and accelerates the development of more efficient energy materials. Single-atom catalysts have relatively well-defined structures, and thus they have been widely investigated by theoretical calculation. In this VSI, the electrochemical CO2 reduction reaction (76−79) and N2 reduction, (80,81) propane dehydrogenation, (82) nonoxidative conversion of methane, (83) and formic acid dehydrogenation (84) on different types of single-atom catalysts have been studied using density functional theory (DFT) simulations. CO oxidation on RuO2(110) surfaces, (85) Pt nanocatalysts, (86) Cu/Rh bimetallic catalysts, (87) Ag clusters, (88) and Ru/RuO2 interfaces (89) is discussed. Theoretical studies on other important reactions are also included: the HER on the VS2 monolayer (90) or black phosphorus, (91) the electrochemical CO2 reduction reaction on h-BN nanosheets, (92) photocatalytic CO2 reduction on silver nanoclusters, (93) CO2 dissociation on Cu clusters, (94) the methanol-to-olefins (MTO) process on different zeolites, (95) the ORR on the two-dimensional NiTe monolayer, (96) the nitrogen reduction reaction on Ru- or Fe-supported Nb2C MXene, (97) NO reduction by CO on Cu2O(110) and Pd1/Cu2O(110), (98) NO oxidation on different oxides, (99) methane steam reforming on Ni, Pt, and Pd, (100) dry reforming of methane on the Ni/Mo alloy, (101) the B2O3-catalyzed oxidative dehydrogenation of propane, (102) the hydrodeoxygenation of hydroquinone on Au/TiO2, (103) the hydrogenation of CO2 to methanol over Pt4/In2O3, (104) syngas conversion to ketene on ZnCr2O4, (105) the structural and optoelectronic properties of ZnSe1–xTex photocatalysts, (106) bimetallic salen-complex-catalyzed epoxide hydration, (107) Rh-catalyzed formaldehyde hydroformylation, (108) the peroxidase and oxidase-like properties of nanoceria in biosystems, (109) the formation of polarons in TiO2, HfO2, and BiVO4, (110,111) and interfacial charge transfer in Li–O2 batteries. (112)
This VSI reflects the recent progress in the experimental and theoretical development of chemical energy in mainland China. It not only focuses on the understanding of the fundamental mechanisms but also involves the development of highly efficient energy materials or devices. A wide range of the hottest topics in the energy field, including hydrogen energy, fuel cells, lithium-ion batteries and other secondary batteries, and electrocatalysis/photocatalysis/thermalcatalysis are covered in this VSI. In situ/operando experimental characterization techniques and theoretical methodologies for the study of these important energy systems are also discussed. We hope this VSI will promote energy research in China as well as communication and collaboration with researchers from other regions.

 SPECIAL ISSUE
Published as part of The Journal of Physical Chemistry virtual special issue “Energy and Catalysis in China”.
Energy plays a central role in our modern society. Due to the growing energy crisis and environmental concerns, seeking clean and sustainable energy sources is becoming one of the most important global issues and has attracted the increasing interest of researchers from all over the world. In particular, research about catalysis in energy, including electrocatalysis, thermocatalysis, and photocatalysis, is an important direction, as it can greatly improve the efficiency of energy storage and conversion or may even create new ways to utilize energy. At the same time, electrochemical energy devices, such as lithium/sodium-ion batteries and fuel cells, have also emerged as highly efficient energy ultilization technologies, especially when being combined with renewable energy power generation. These devices involve complicated physical chemistry processes. Therefore, understanding the fundamental physical chemistry mechanisms of these energy-related processes will promote the development of more efficient functional materials and/or energy devices. During the past decade, researchers in China have made extensive efforts and achieved significant advances in the energy and catalysis field. This Virtual Special Issue (VSI) highlights the recent experimental and theoretical progress made by researchers from mainland China. It includes 112 papers, which cover topics from the development of highly efficient energy materials to in situ/operando characterization, mechanism studies, and theoretical simulations.
Electrocatalysis and electrochemical energy generation/storage are essential for the utilization of renewable energy like solar and wind energy, as they can help to store these intermittent energies as chemical energy. The prototypical electrochemical energy devices are fuel cells and lithium-ion batteries. The oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) is the most critical electrocatalytic reaction in fuel cells due to its slow kinetics. This VSI includes 7 research papers about the development of efficient or stable ORR catalysts, ultrathin palladium/transition-metal alloy nanowires, (1) Au-modified Pt–Co/C, (2) PtnNi tetrahedrons, (3)N,N-di(2-picolyl)ethylenediamine (DPEN)-modified Co corrole, (4) Fe–Nx–C or Co–N4 single-atom catalysts, (5,6) and Co-, N-, and S-tridoped carbon frameworks for ORR (7) as well as a review paper about non-precious-metal electrocatalysts. (8) Moreover, the influence of the microstructures and transport properties of ionomer films on the performance of Pt single atom catalysts (SACs) in polymer electrolyte membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs) has been reported. (9) The development of electrode materials for lithium-ion batteries is included in this VSI: lithium-iron-phosphate-coated LiNixCoyMn1–x–yO2 (NCM) (10) and porous Co3O4 nanoparticles assembled nanostructures for lithium-ion batteries, (11) and N,S-codoped three-dimensional (3D) network carbon materials supported RuO2 for Li–O2 batteries, (12) while Fe-doped PrBa0.8Ca0.2Co2O6−δ double perovskite oxide is reported as an efficient cathode material for solid oxide fuel cells. (13) The understanding of reactive oxygen species in aprotic lithium–oxygen batteries is reviewed. (14) There are also several papers concerning other important/emerging electrocatalytic reactions and energy processes: the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) on a carbon-nanotube-supported NiIrx nanoalloy, (15) NiO/CuO nanosheet, (16) or Ni3S2/Fe(OH)2, (17) the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) on WNi4@W–WO2, (18) CO2 reduction on a 3D porous Zn catalyst, (19) oxygen reduction to hydrogen peroxide on a Nd-doped Bi4Ti3O12 nanosheet, (20) the reduction of N2 to ammonia on a Au/SiO2/Si photocathode, (21) the selective oxidation of methane to ethanol on Rh/ZnO nanosheets, (22) the triiodine reduction reaction on zeolitic-imidazolate-framework-derived Co–N3 SACs, (23) the electrohydrogenation of cinnamate esters on graphite felt electrode, (24) and electricity generation via the hydrovoltaic effect. (25)
Thermocatalysis and Photocatalysis. Thermocatalysis improves the utilization efficiency of traditional fossil energy or reduces the emission of pollutants. In this VSI, various thermocatalytic reactions on catalysts of metals, oxides, carbon materials, zeolites, resins, and others are discussed. These papers can be roughly classified as follows. (1) Energy catalysis: conversion of syngas to higher alcohols on CoMn-modified Cu-based mixed oxides, (26) direct methane conversion to methanol on PdAu nanowires, (27) hydrogenation of CO2 to ethanol on Na-promoted Rh embedded in S-1 zeolites, (28) oxidative coupling of methane on A+1Nb5+O3 (A = Li, Na, K) perovskites, (29) and synthesis of jet fuel from glycerol and tert-butyl alcohol on organic solid acid catalysts. (30) (2) Environmental catalysis: catalytic elimination of soot and NOx on Mn-based perovskites, (31) catalytic dehydrochlorination of dichloroethane on porous carbon, (32) oxidation of propane and CO on reducible oxide-supported Pt or PtCu nanocatalysts, (33,34) complete oxidation of benzene on Pt SACs, (35) preferential oxidation of CO on CuxCe1–xO2 nanorods, (36) CO oxidation on Pd/ZnO catalysts (37) or Au–Fe2O3 interfaces, (38) and selective catalytic reduction of nitrogen oxide with methane on Co-exchanged SSZ-13 zeolite catalysts. (39) (3) Synthesis of fine chemicals: selective reduction of nitrobenzene on iron and nitrogen cofunctionalized carbon materials (40) or PtPdCu/Al2O3, (41) hydrogenation of quinoline and benzoic acid on RhPt/MCM-41, (42) hydroformylation of diisobutene on CoFe alloy catalysts, (43) and selective hydrogenation of cinnamaldehyde to cinnamyl alcohol on Pt@Fe-CeO2 catalysts (44) or Pt/TiO2. (45) Photocatalysis can directly convert solar energy into chemical energy, and two important photocatalytic reactions are included in this VSI: photocatalytic water splitting on Au nanoparticles embedded in g-C3N4, (46) BiVO4, (47) Ta3N5, (48) SnNb2O6 nanoplates, (49) silicon material, (50) MoO3–ZnIn2S4, (51) carbon nitride, (52) NiFe metal–organic framework (MOF) and cobaloxime-modified Ti-doped hematite, (53) and photocatalytic CO2 conversion on α-Fe2O3/CdS heterostructures, (54) CdSe/CdSexS1–x/CdS alloyed quantum dots/TiO2, (55) AgTaO3, (56) perylene diimide/graphene-g-C3N4, (57) P-doped ZnIn2S4, (58) MCo2O4−δ (M = Zn, Ni, Cu), (59) and PtRu/TiO2. (60) A few other photocatalytic reactions, such as the selective oxidation of cyclohexane to cyclohexanone on In2O3/N-doped TiO2, (61) the reduction of aromatic nitro compounds on Ag/AgxS, (62) and NO removal on Ba-doped BiOBr, (63) are also discussed.
In situ characterization can provide fundamental insights into reaction mechanisms and structure–activity relationships of energy processes, which promote the rational design of more efficient energy materials and devices. In this VSI, nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy has been used to investigate the surface acidity of niobium and tantalum oxides (64) and the structure evolution of Na3V2(PO4)3, a typical representative of Na-ion batteries, under different charging and discharging states, (65) as well as the photocatalytic methanol or ethanol reforming reaction on photocatalysts consisting of Pt supported on graphitic carbon nitride. (66) In situ infrared spectroscopy was employed to study the reaction mechanism of electrocatalysis. (67) In situ X-ray absorption spectroscopy was used to study the lithium-ion battery cathode materials (68) and the OER on α-Ni(OH)2 nanobelts. (69) Near-ambient-pressure X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and quasi-in-situ XPS were used to study oxygen vacancies in reduced MnOx (70) and CO activation and hydrogenation on Co(0001), (71) respectively. Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) was used to characterize the self-assembled Pd(II) meso-tetra(4-carboxyphenyl)porphine (PdTCPP) adlayer under electrochemical conditions, (72) the FeO/Cu2O/Cu(100) interfaces during thermal reduction, (73) and the structural changes of FeO/Cu(111) during redox treatments. (74) Differential electrochemical mass spectrometry was used in the study of the stability of organic cation species (R4N+, R = CH3–, C2H5–, and n-C3H7−) during the HER. (75)
Theoretical calculation can directly simulate the chemical and electronic properties of energy materials as well as their interactions with reactants, intermediates, and products. It greatly deepens the understanding of the molecular mechanisms of various energy processes and accelerates the development of more efficient energy materials. Single-atom catalysts have relatively well-defined structures, and thus they have been widely investigated by theoretical calculation. In this VSI, the electrochemical CO2 reduction reaction (76−79) and N2 reduction, (80,81) propane dehydrogenation, (82) nonoxidative conversion of methane, (83) and formic acid dehydrogenation (84) on different types of single-atom catalysts have been studied using density functional theory (DFT) simulations. CO oxidation on RuO2(110) surfaces, (85) Pt nanocatalysts, (86) Cu/Rh bimetallic catalysts, (87) Ag clusters, (88) and Ru/RuO2 interfaces (89) is discussed. Theoretical studies on other important reactions are also included: the HER on the VS2 monolayer (90) or black phosphorus, (91) the electrochemical CO2 reduction reaction on h-BN nanosheets, (92) photocatalytic CO2 reduction on silver nanoclusters, (93) CO2 dissociation on Cu clusters, (94) the methanol-to-olefins (MTO) process on different zeolites, (95) the ORR on the two-dimensional NiTe monolayer, (96) the nitrogen reduction reaction on Ru- or Fe-supported Nb2C MXene, (97) NO reduction by CO on Cu2O(110) and Pd1/Cu2O(110), (98) NO oxidation on different oxides, (99) methane steam reforming on Ni, Pt, and Pd, (100) dry reforming of methane on the Ni/Mo alloy, (101) the B2O3-catalyzed oxidative dehydrogenation of propane, (102) the hydrodeoxygenation of hydroquinone on Au/TiO2, (103) the hydrogenation of CO2 to methanol over Pt4/In2O3, (104) syngas conversion to ketene on ZnCr2O4, (105) the structural and optoelectronic properties of ZnSe1–xTex photocatalysts, (106) bimetallic salen-complex-catalyzed epoxide hydration, (107) Rh-catalyzed formaldehyde hydroformylation, (108) the peroxidase and oxidase-like properties of nanoceria in biosystems, (109) the formation of polarons in TiO2, HfO2, and BiVO4, (110,111) and interfacial charge transfer in Li–O2 batteries. (112)
This VSI reflects the recent progress in the experimental and theoretical development of chemical energy in mainland China. It not only focuses on the understanding of the fundamental mechanisms but also involves the development of highly efficient energy materials or devices. A wide range of the hottest topics in the energy field, including hydrogen energy, fuel cells, lithium-ion batteries and other secondary batteries, and electrocatalysis/photocatalysis/thermalcatalysis are covered in this VSI. In situ/operando experimental characterization techniques and theoretical methodologies for the study of these important energy systems are also discussed. We hope this VSI will promote energy research in China as well as communication and collaboration with researchers from other regions.
