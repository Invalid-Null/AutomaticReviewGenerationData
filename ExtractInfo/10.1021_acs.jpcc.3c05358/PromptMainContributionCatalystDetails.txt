Structure Sensitivity Study of Propane Dehydrogenation on Cobalt Catalysts: HCP versus FCC
Abstract
In heterogeneous catalysis, it is crucial to understand the structure sensitivity in order to elucidate the reaction mechanism and rationally design optimal catalysts. In this work, propane dehydrogenation (PDH) and side reactions (cracking and deep dehydrogenation) were studied by density functional theory calculations on Co catalysts with different crystallographic structures: face-centered cubic (FCC) and hexagonal closed packed (HCP). Wulff construction reveals that the most stable facet of each crystallographic structure, viz. Co(111) and Co(0001), cover 76.2% and 19.4% of the exposed surface. Various reaction pathways for PDH, deep dehydrogenation, and cracking were explored on Co(111) and Co(0001). Microkinetic simulation results suggest that PDH proceeds through both dehydrogenation pathways (via 1-propyl or 2-propyl intermediate) on Co(111), while it favors path A (via 1-propyl intermediate) on Co(0001). At typical reaction conditions of PDH, Co(111) is more active than Co(0001) by 1.3 times, while the latter is more selective toward propylene production and more resistive to coke formation. Compared to Pt(111), both Co surfaces are more active for PDH while Co(0001) is also more selective toward propylene formation. This work provides fundamental insights into the crystallographic structure sensitivity of propane dehydrogenation on a Co catalyst and useful guidance to achieve better catalytic performance for a Co catalyst.

1. Introduction
ARTICLE SECTIONSJump To
Propylene is an important chemical raw material, and its downstream products of great variety are closely related to peopleâ€™s life. Driven by this, the demand for basic organic chemical products generated from propylene has soared, and the propane to propylene process solves both the propane surplus and the propylene shortage problem. (1,2) As a new process for the production of propylene, direct dehydrogenation of propane has the advantages of high yield and less byproducts compared with the traditional steam cracking process. (3) Given the good economic prospect of this process, propane dehydrogenation (PDH) has attracted extensive attention from the catalysis community to develop more efficient heterogeneous catalysts than the commercial Pt and CrOx catalysts used in industry. (4âˆ’8) As shown in Scheme 1, competing side reactions of PDH involve the deep dehydrogenation of propylene and the cracking of the C3 species. According to the sequence of dehydrogenation, there are two possible reaction pathways for PDH: one is to eliminate H on the primary carbon first and form a 1-propyl (CH3CH2CH2*) intermediate (path A), and the other is to eliminate H on the secondary carbon first and form a 2-propyl (CH3CHCH3*) intermediate (path B). Similarly, deep dehydrogenation reactions leading to propyne formation can also occur through two pathways via 1-propenyl (CH3CHCH3*, path D) and 2-propenyl (CH3CHCH2*, path E) intermediates, respectively. In the process of dehydrogenation, any C3 species may have a side reaction caused by cleavage of the Câ€“C bond. Both of these side reactions will lead to coking and eventually to the deactivation of the surface active centers of the catalyst.

Determining the structural sensitivity of catalytic reactions is one critical step for developing structureâ€“activity relationship and designing optimal catalysts in heterogeneous catalysis. (9) A typical example of structurally sensitive reactions is the Fischerâ€“Tropsch synthesis (FTS). (10âˆ’12) As the rate-determining step in FTS, CO activation is greatly impacted by the crystallographic structures and exposed crystal facets of Co, (13) Ru (14) and Ni (15) catalysts. For Co, phase transition between the hexagonal close-packed (HCP) phase and the face centered cubic (FCC) phase can occur by tuning the catalyst size, support, and by varying the pretreating conditions (or reaction conditions). (16) HCP-Co is more active than FCC-Co in FTS due to lower activation energies of CO dissociation. (17) CO activation follows different reaction mechanisms on different facets of HCP-Co, among which the (101Ì…1) facet shows the highest FTS activity as well as carbon chain growth ability to form C5+ products. (18âˆ’20)
With the complexity of the phase transition of Co during reaction conditions, structure sensitivity of PDH on Co catalysts is still an open question. Chen et al. (21) prepared ultrafine Co particles in dealuminated Î² zeolite, the catalytic performance of which is comparable to the commercial catalysts with 57% propane conversion and 96% propylene selectivity being achieved at 600 Â°C for PDH. They also suggested that PDH over Co is likely a structure-sensitive reaction with small confined Co particles being the active sites, whereas large Co and Co3O4 particles lead to cracking and carbon deposition side reactions. Wang et al. (22) prepared single-atom Co catalysts within the three-dimensional channels of SiO2 nanomeshes (NMs), which exhibited superior PDH performance of 95% propylene selectivity and 196 hâ€“1 turnover frequency (TOF). They indicated that the unsaturated Coâ€“O3 sites are the dehydrogenation sites that account for the 15 times higher activity than Co nanoparticles supported on SiO2 NMs. But none of these studies investigated the sensitivity of PDH regarding the Co crystallographic structures.
The crystal-structure sensitivity of PDH on other metals was rarely discussed in the literature, where most relevant studies focused on activity dependence on catalyst size or crystal planes. Take the most extensively studied Pt catalyst as an example, Zhang et al. (23) demonstrated that the activity, selectivity toward propylene and stability of Pt/Al2O3 catalysts for PDH are significantly dependent on the size of platinum. Yang et al. studied the mechanism of PDH on flat Pt(111) and stepped Pt(211) surfaces using density functional theory (DFT) calculations. (24) They found the activity of Pt(111) for PDH is lower than that of Pt(211), while propylene selectivity on Pt(111) is higher than Pt(211) by restraining the cracking of C3 species. As deep dehydrogenation and cracking of C3 derivatives proceed, the stepped surface preferentially forms coke. This explains the experimental results that smaller Pt nanoparticles with more step sites are more active and less selective in PDH than larger ones.
Researchers have investigated the reaction mechanism of PDH on Pt- and Ni-based (25) catalysts in detail with computational studies, providing useful guidance to the rational design of superior catalysts. But this does not include Co-based catalysts, where the detailed reaction mechanism of PDH remains to be elucidated. In this work, we studied PDH together with the deep dehydrogenation and cracking side reactions on FCC-Co and HCP-Co, revealing the structure-sensitive nature of this reaction, regarding the Co crystallographic structure.

3.1. Wulff Construction
Cobalt metal has two distinct crystal phases, FCC-Co and HCP-Co. The contribution of a single crystal to the overall reaction activity is affected by the surface-area ratio of exposed facets, since the intrinsic activity of each facet is different. The equilibrium morphology of FCC-Co and HCP-Co with multiple exposed crystal planes based on the Wulff construction can be obtained from the calculated surface energy, as shown in Figure 1.

3.2. Adsorption of H, Propane, Propylene, and Propyne on Co
The reactant and product C3 species (propane, propylene, propyne) and the generated H atoms during dehydrogenation steps are important to the overall reaction; therefore, their adsorption on Co surfaces are discussed first. Literature reports have proved that the PBE functional does not describe the nonlocal van der Waals (vdW) interactions well. (37,38) Here, we use the DFT-D3 method to account for the vdW forces. (39)
There are three possible adsorption sites for hydrogen on both Co surfaces, namely, fcc, hcp, and top sites (Figure S2a). Table S1 summarizes the adsorption energies of H at different positions on both Co surfaces. The fcc site is the most stable adsorption position for H on both Co(111) and Co(0001) (Figure 2), with an adsorption energy of âˆ’3.90 and âˆ’3.98 eV, respectively. The hcp site is slightly less favorable than the fcc site, while the top site is the least favorable for H adsorption. H does not adsorb on the top sites of Co(111), and it binds weakly on the top site of Co(0001), with an Eads of âˆ’3.45 eV. Compared to Pt(111) with an Eads of âˆ’3.92 eV for H adsorption on the most stable fcc site, H binds slightly more strongly on Co(0001) (Table 2).

3.3. Bader Charge and Electronic Structure Calculations
We calculated the Bader charge of adsorbed propane, propylene, and propyne on Co(111) and Co(0001). Our results show that upon adsorption of propane, propylene, and propyne on Co surfaces, electron will transfer from Co to adsorbates, rendering them negatively charged. The calculated charge transfer and the differential charge density caused by adsorption are presented in Table S5 and Figure S4, respectively.
Bader charge analysis shows that both Co(111) and Co(0001) crystals lose electrons to C3 adsorbates, but the latter loses more than the former in general. The number of electrons gains by C3 adsorbates follows the order of propyne > propylene > propane. It clearly shows the greater the adsorption strength is, the more charge is transferred between Co and adsorbates. For example, Co(111) and Co(0001) only lose 0.04 and 0.03 electrons, respectively, to the weakly adsorbed propane. Propyne adsorbs most strongly among the three C3 species, and it also gained the highest number of electrons, viz. 0.50 electrons from Co(111) and 0.58 electrons from Co(0001). In fact, we found a good linear relationship (R2 = 0.79) between the adsorption energy of C3 adsorbates and the amount of charge transferred to them from Co (Figure 3). Therefore, the charge transfer is a good descriptor for the adsorption strength of C3 species on Co.

For path A, adsorbed propane removes the first hydrogen from its primary carbon and forms 1-propyl adsorbed to the top site on Co(111) and hcp site on Co(0001); the corresponding activation energies are 0.73 and 1.05 eV, respectively. The produced 1-propyl intermediate adsorbs on the surface in a more compact manner than propane, and its adsorption energy is âˆ’3.53 eV and âˆ’3.32 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively. The second dehydrogenation step of path A removes a hydrogen from the secondary carbon and occurs at the off-top position with very small barriers of 0.27 and 0.13 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively. Comparing the activation energies of the two dehydrogenation steps, the second step is a facile reaction while the first dehydrogenation step is the rate-limiting step (RLS) of PDH for path A.
For path B, dehydrogenation of propane initiates from the secondary carbon with activation energies of 0.66 and 1.15 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively. This barrier is higher than the corresponding value for path A on Co(0001), while the opposite is true for Co(111). The 2-propyl formed by dehydrogenation still forms a strong chemical bond with the catalyst surface, and its adsorption energy is âˆ’3.44 and âˆ’2.25 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively. 2-propyl is less stable than its isomer, 1-propyl generated in path A. Compared to the first dehydrogenation step, the second dehydrogenation step takes place on the primary carbon with much smaller respective barriers of 0.35 and 0.36 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001). In common with path A, the first dehydrogenation step of path B has a higher barrier than the second and serves as the rate-limiting step of this pathway.
As shown in Figure 4, path A is more favorable than path B on the Co(0001) catalyst, where the former has lower energies for all states on the PED than the latter. On the contrary, Co(111) prefers path B as its PDH reaction path. Compared to Pt(111), all elementary reactions leading to propylene formation have smaller barriers on the Co(111) catalyst, especially, the second dehydrogenation reactions (dehydrogenation of 1-propyl and 2-propyl) occur more easily on Co(111) than on Pt(111) (Table 2). Focusing on the RLS of the more preferable pathway, viz. the first dehydrogenation step of the optimal path, Co(111) has a smaller barrier than Pt(111) by 0.11 eV, while Co(0001) has a higher barrier than Pt(111) by 0.28 eV. This indicates that Co(111) is probably more active for PDH than Pt(111), while Co(0001) is less active than Pt(111).
Desorption of the produced propylene from PDH will compete with the side reactions, including deep dehydrogenation and cracking, determining the selectivity of propylene. The calculated desorption energies (or desorption barriers) of propylene on Co(111) and Co(0001) are 2.25 and 1.37 eV respectively, which are the opposite numbers of Eads for propylene presented in Table 2. The side reactions and selectivity of propylene are discussed in the following sections.

3.5.1. Deep Dehydrogenation Reactions to Form Propyne
For the two deep dehydrogenation pathways, path D is to remove a hydrogen atom from the terminal carbon at the double bond end and forms 1-propenyl, followed by hydrogen removal from the middle carbon to form propyne, while path E has a reverse dehydrogenation sequence via the 2-propenyl intermediate. All intermediate and transition state structures of deep dehydrogenation are shown in Figure 6. The first dehydrogenation step in path D has modest barriers of 0.79 and 0.64 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively, while the second dehydrogenation step needs to overcome respective barriers of 1.19 and 0.37 eV. As a result, the first dehydrogenation step represents the rate-limiting step for path D on Co(0001) and second dehydrogenation step for Co(111).
The first dehydrogenation step in path E (step E3) has respective barriers of 0.86 and 0.64 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), while the second step (step E4) has respective barriers of 1.32 and 0.29 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001). Similar to path D, the first and the second dehydrogenation step serve as the RLS for path E on Co(0001) and Co(111), respectively.
Comparing the energetics of the two deep dehydrogenation pathways on PED in Figure 4, both Co(111) and Co(0001) prefer path E as the deep-dehydrogenation pathway. Co(0001) is more active than Co(111) for the deep dehydrogenation reactions to form propyne, and it is also more active than Pt(111) for the deep dehydrogenation reactions.
3.5.2. Cracking of C3 Species
The Câ€“C, Câ•C and Câ‰¡C bonds usually have very high bond energies and need to surmount high barriers to break the bonds. Generally speaking, the strong interaction between the catalyst surface and adsorbates can weaken these Câ€“C bonds. As a weakly adsorbed species, propane cracking to ethyl (CH3CH2*) and methyl (CH3*) has very high respective barriers of 1.71 and 2.69 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), which are much higher than the dehydrogenation barriers of any path. Such difficult activation of propane occurs due to the steric hindrance effect of H on tetrahedral methyl groups, which hinders the contact of propane C atoms with surface Co atoms.
Cleavage of 1-propyl to methylene (CH2*) and ethyl (CH3CH2*) has a barrier of 1.13 and 1.15 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively; the Câ€“C activation of 1-propyl is more difficult than its dehydrogenation on both Co surfaces. The Câ€“C cleavage of 2-propyl to methyl and ethylidene (CH3CH*) also has a higher barrier (1.05 and 0.98 eV) than its dehydrogenation on Co catalysts.
Câ•C bond cleavage of propylene forming methylene (CH2*) and ethylidene (CH3CH*) has activation energies of 2.21 and 1.10 eV on Co(111) and Co(0001), respectively. Propylene dehydrogenation is more easily activated than cracking of propylene, and therefore, deep dehydrogenation is the major side reaction that diminishes the propylene selectivity.
For the cracking of 1-propenyl and 2-propenyl intermediates, only the cracking of 1-propenyl on Co(111) has a similar barrier as its dehydrogenation (Figure 8a,b), while all the other cracking steps are more difficult than the corresponding dehydrogenation steps. Compared to Pt(111), cracking on Co catalysts are in general more facile besides a few exceptions related to closed-shell species. Cracking of propane and propyne on Co(0001) are more difficult than on Pt(111), while cracking of propylene and propyne on Co(111) are more difficult than on Pt(111). Comparing the cracking barriers with dehydrogenation barriers, we can conclude that the most probable precursor for coke formation is 1-propenyl on Co(111) and Pt(111), and 2-propenyl on Co(0001).

3.6. Propylene Selectivity Analysis
The target product of the PDH process is propylene, the selectivity of which will be diminished by deep dehydrogenation and cracking side reactions. As shown in Figure 8c, cracking has a higher activation energy than dehydrogenation for most species on both Co(111) and Co(0001). The only exception is 1-propenyl on Co(111); viz., its cracking barrier is lower than its dehydrogenation barrier by 0.01 eV. We note 1-propenyl belongs to path D that is less favorable and may carry minimal reaction flux, and this exception will not affect the selectivity of propylene significantly. In addition, cracking side reactions most likely occur at a very late stage after propylene formation, through 1-propenyl or 2-propneyl intermediate. Therefore, deep dehydrogenation is the dominant side reaction that impacts propylene selectivity on both Co(111) and Co(0001). In order to compare the propylene selectivity on the two Co surfaces, we define the energy difference (Î´E) between desorption energy and dehydrogenation barrier of propylene, Î´E = Edesorption â€“ Ea, dehydrogenation. The larger the Î´E value, the lower the propylene selectivity. As shown in Figure 8, parts a and b, propylene preferentially goes through the deep dehydrogenation reaction among the three possible reaction pathways (desorption, deep dehydrogenation, and cracking) on both Co surfaces. As a result, both Co surfaces have positive Î´E values, 1.46 eV for Co(111) and 0.72 eV for Co(0001), the former is less selective toward propylene formation than the latter. Pt(111) has a Î´E value of 1.00 eV, indicating Co(0001) is more propylene selective than Co(111) and Pt(111). The same conclusion is reached when using the corresponding Gibbs free energy values at a typical reaction temperature of 853 K (Table S8).
Based on the DFT-calculated energies presented in Figure 8d, Co(111) surface is more active than Pt(111) for PDH although its propylene selectivity is less satisfying, while Co(0001) is more selective toward propylene production with lower activity than Pt(111). Adding metal promoters (such as Sn, (43) Ga, (44) Y (45)) has been proven to be useful strategies to promote the propylene selectivity on Pt-based catalysts. Experimental and computational studies both reveal that introducing Sn to Pt forming Ptâ€“Sn alloys significantly improves the propylene selectivity without compensating much of its activity. Likewise, this strategy could be applied to a more active Co catalyst to elevate its propylene selectivity.
3.7. Microkinetic Analysis
We also performed microkinetic analysis with the CatMAP (46) software using the DFT acquired data. The reaction model includes all eight dehydrogenation steps shown in Table 3 together with the adsorption of propane and the desorption of various products. Please see Supporting Information, Section 2, for detailed information about the microkinetic simulation.
We plotted and compared the turnover frequency (TOF) of propane dehydrogenation on Co(111), Co(0001) and Pt(111) as a function of temperature or pressure (Figure 9). As shown in Figure 9A, both Co(111) and Co(0001) have higher reaction rates than Pt(111) at industrial relevant reaction temperatures above 750 K. Co(111) is more sensitive to the reaction temperature than the other two catalysts and becomes the most active catalyst at higher temperature above 850 K, while Co(0001) is the most active one at lower temperature below 800 K. The reaction rates can be promoted at higher pressure on all three catalysts. Co(0001) is the most sensitive catalyst to reaction pressure: its TOF for PDH increases by ca. 4.57 times from 1 to 5 bar. At typical reaction conditions of 853 K and 1 bar, the TOF of PDH on Co(111) and Co(0001) is higher than Pt(111) by 7.54 and 5.97 times, respectively. We note the higher activity of Co(0001) (than Pt(111)) for PDH is not expected based on the DFT-obtained PEDs, which signifies the temperature effects on reaction energetics. The corresponding Gibbs free energy (at 853 K) diagram is shown in Figure S7.

4. Conclusions
ARTICLE SECTIONSJump To
In this work, we used density functional theory calculations to reveal the reaction mechanism and the structure-sensitive nature of PDH on Co catalysts. The equilibrium morphology of FCC-Co from Wulff construction has an octahedron-like shape with eight most stable (111) planes, while HCP-Co has a dihedron-like shape with two most stable (0001) planes. Then the reaction mechanism of PDH together with side reactions of deep dehydrogenation and cracking were studied in detail on Co(111) and Co(0001).
Based on microkinetic simulation results, PDH preferentially occurs through path A via the 1-propyl intermediate on Co(0001), while both dehydrogenation pathways contribute to the reaction flux on Co(111). For the side deep dehydrogenation reactions, path E via the 2-propenyl intermediate is favored on both Co(111) and Co(0001). Cracking most likely takes place at a late stage of the reaction after propylene formation. The most probable coke precursors are 1-propenyl on Co(111) and 2-propenyl on Co(0001). Co(111) is more prone to coke deposition than Co(0001). Due to the high cracking barriers, deep dehydrogenation is the dominant side reaction that competes with propylene desorption.
At typical reaction conditions of 853 K and 1 bar, both Co(111) and Co(0001) are more active than Pt(111) with higher TOF for the PDH reaction by 7.54 and 5.97 times, respectively. The selectivity toward propylene production follows the trend of Co(0001) > Pt(111) > Co111). Given the excellent PDH activity of Co compared to commercial catalysts, careful catalyst design to selectively prohibit the deep dehydrogenation reactions is highly desirable for its practical application. In this regard, this work provides fundamental mechanistic insights that are of great significance for the rational design of optimal Co-based catalysts for propane dehydrogenation.
